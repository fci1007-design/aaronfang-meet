Research Report on Poland's Local Content Policy and EV / Bus Regulations

An investigation into whether the Polish government requires electric vehicles or buses to be manufactured in Poland, whether Chinese companies must establish local production facilities, and whether the rumored "51% parts must originate from Poland" claim is valid.

↓
Executive Summary
The Polish government's policy is not about excluding foreign investment, but rather incorporating "local participation" as a scoring advantage in state-owned procurement. Poland currently has no hard legal threshold requiring electric vehicles or electric buses to be manufactured in Poland, nor any requirement that 51% of parts must originate from Poland.

In response to the question, "Does the Polish government require electric vehicles or buses to be manufactured in Poland, particularly requiring Chinese companies to establish factories in Poland, or that 51% of product parts must originate from Poland?" — the following is a comprehensive research summary based on official announcements from Poland's Ministry of State Assets (MAP) and related reports.

Conclusion 1 No mandatory requirement for foreign companies to manufacture in Poland The official definitions found to date focus on "local participation" assessment, not sales qualification restrictions.
Conclusion 2 "51% parts must be produced in Poland" is a misinterpretation The "over 50%" threshold appearing in the policy measures a company's connection to the Polish economy, not the origin of product parts.
Conclusion 3 Foreign investors can still bid, but higher local engagement offers a scoring advantage If a company wishes to improve its chances of winning bids, the focus should be on strengthening its legal presence, employment, taxation, and operational footprint in Poland — rather than forcibly changing global parts sourcing.
Regulation Check

1. No Mandatory Requirement for Foreign Companies to Manufacture in Poland

The first point to clarify is whether the Local Content direction announced by MAP has already established "setting up production in Poland" as a legal prerequisite for foreign companies.

According to a press release published by Poland's Ministry of State Assets (MAP) on February 19, 2026, the government introduced the "Local Content" concept aimed at increasing the participation of Polish enterprises in major investment projects by state-owned companies.Source

Official Definition

Adopts a two-tier definition, not a threshold

The document indicates that the authorities use "macro-level" and "operational-level" definitions to measure local content, emphasizing that these definitions must be inclusive across industries and must not exclude any enterprise.Source

Policy Implication

Core policy objective is to encourage local supply chains

This means the policy centers on increasing the share of Polish enterprises in strategic investments, steering procurement decisions toward supporting the local economy, employment, and tax base — rather than directly excluding foreign investment from the market.

Key Conclusion

No clauses mandating local production for market access have been found

To date, no law or explicit administrative order has been identified requiring foreign companies to first establish factories in Poland before being allowed to sell EVs or electric buses. Therefore, Local Content should not be directly equated with "no local production, no sales."

Rumor Analysis

2. "51% Parts Must Be Produced in Poland" Is a Misinterpretation

The second focus is whether the market rumor of a 51% parts localization requirement stems from legislation or from a misunderstanding of MAP's scoring logic.

Source Tracing

The rumor likely stems from the "over half" threshold in the scoring indicators being misinterpreted

  • On April 9, 2026, MAP published an article titled "Local Content. For the Benefit of Poland," further explaining the methodology for assessing a company's "Polishness."Source
  • The article lists six indicators for evaluating a company's "Polishness," including the location of the parent company headquarters, primary place of business, whether taxes are paid in Poland, whether employees are predominantly Polish residents and taxpayers, registration and continuous operation in Poland for more than three years, and whether more than half of annual revenue is generated in Poland.Source
  • The two highest-weighted criteria — "parent company headquarters location" and "primary place of business" — each account for 25%. These criteria serve as procurement scoring references, not parts origin requirements.Source
Over-50% Threshold

"Over half" refers to corporate ties, not product BOM

Several indicators mention an "over 50%" ratio — for example, over half of employees or revenue being Poland-related. These figures assess a company's connection to the Polish economy, not the origin of product parts.Source

Market Interpretation

"51% parts" is most likely a market rumor

Therefore, the circulating claim that "51% of parts must be produced in Poland" is more likely a misunderstanding of the indicator design rather than an official requirement published by MAP.

Practical Impact

No mandatory EV or bus parts ratio requirement has been identified

Based on all official MAP announcements and related reports reviewed to date, there is no hard requirement for any specific parts ratio for electric vehicles or electric buses.

Recommendations

3. Impact and Strategic Recommendations

If the goal is to participate in Polish government or state-owned company electric bus procurement in the future, the real focus should not be on the parts origin narrative, but on how to enhance "Polishness" within the local scoring framework.

Policy Effect

The policy does not block foreign investment; it steers procurement toward localization

The "Local Content" policy focuses on encouraging state-owned enterprises to prioritize cooperation with Polish companies, using procurement strategies to foster local supply chains and employment. However, MAP also emphasizes that the policy must comply with EU public procurement regulations.Source

Bidding Logic

Foreign companies can still participate in tenders

Foreign companies are not excluded from bidding; however, if their local engagement scores are low, they may be at a disadvantage during evaluation. In other words, the competitiveness gap is more likely to come from scoring rather than eligibility.

Operational Adjustments

Improve chances of winning through local presence

  • Establish a subsidiary or assembly plant in Poland
  • Hire Polish residents and build a local management team
  • Pay taxes in Poland, creating a clear local operational track record
Strategic Insight

Parts can be globally sourced, but the company must demonstrate local commitment

Even if product parts are primarily supplied from other regions, as long as the company can clearly demonstrate a commitment to deepening its presence in the Polish market, it still has an opportunity to achieve a better position under MAP's scoring logic.

Interpretation

This scoring system is a procurement bonus, not a legal obligation

Companies do not need to reach any specific score to participate in tenders, but the higher the score, the greater the advantage in state-owned enterprise evaluations. Therefore, the most important thing for enterprises is not to pursue a non-existent parts-ratio threshold, but to assess which local investments can effectively increase their score and win rate.

Summary

Based on a comprehensive review of MAP official announcements and related reports, Poland does not mandate that electric vehicles or electric buses must be manufactured in Poland, nor does it require that 51% of parts must originate from Poland. The rumor more likely stems from the "over half" threshold used by MAP to measure a company's "local engagement," but this is an assessment of the enterprise's economic ties to Poland, not an inspection of product parts origin. Going forward, if a company considers participating in Polish public procurement, it is recommended to gradually increase its operational, employment, and tax presence in Poland to improve scoring, and to continuously monitor MAP's subsequent policy updates.

Decision Point

If the goal is to compete for Polish state-owned electric bus tenders in the future, the real priority is not to prove that 51% of parts come from Poland, but whether the company is willing to use legal presence, employment, taxation, and local operations to transform itself from a mere exporter into a supply partner with genuine connections to the Polish economy.
